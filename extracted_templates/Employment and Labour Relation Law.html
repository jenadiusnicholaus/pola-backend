1. What Is Employment?

Employment is a work relationsh ip between two people ( an employer and an employee) where the employee agrees to perform work or services under control and direction of the employer, in exchange for an agreed salary /wages and/ or other compensations from the employer.

2. Who is an Employer?

An employer is a person or organization that hires another person to do work or service, and usually pays a salary/ wages or other compensation for that work. In simple language: An employer is someone who gives out work to be done and then pays for the work that is done for them.

3. Who is an Employee?

An employee is a person who agrees to work for an other person ( employer ) under specific terms, in exchange for a salary or other forms of payment s and compensation . In simple language: An employee is a person who works for someone or an organization and is paid for the work they do.

4. Employees Rights

The following are the rights of an employee according to the Employment and Labor Relations Law;

i. The right to Obtain an Employment Contract

It is the right of every employee to have an empl oyment contract. The contract will be considered as a legal agreement between an employer and an employee, which provides directions regarding the particular employment, taking into account all rights and responsibilities of both parties.

i i . The Right to Written Particulars of Employment

At the start of employment, an employee is entitled to a written statement of particulars, either separately or within the contract. This should include your name, age, address, sex, job description, place and date of commencement, contract type/duration, workplace, work hours, remuneration, benefits, disciplinary and grievance procedures and other prescribed details. If the contract covers all of these, then the employer is not required to prepare written Employment Instructions . Employers must retain these records for at least five years after termination.

i i i . The Right to Know Your Rights

Employers are required to display a notice of employee rights in a visible place at the workplace. These rights include s, but is not limited to freedom of association, leave entitlements, overtime pay, rest periods, certificate of service upon termination, subsistence allowances, and more.

iv . Freedom of Association

Employees have the right to form, join, and participate in trade unions. Discrimination for union activities is prohibited. Similarly, unions and employer associations enjoy r ights to organize and affiliate .

v . Leave Entitlements

An employee has the right to paid Annual leave, Sick Leave, Maternity Leave, Parte nity Leave, Public Holidays and Rest days . Unpaid leave of up to 30 days with employer’s written consent has also been added to employees leave according to 2025 Labour Law (Amendments) Act No4 .

v i . Fair Remuneration & Overtime Pay

An employee has the right t o be paid wages and overtime as required by law .

v i i . Health & Safety Rights

Under the Occupation al Health and Safety Act , and the Employment Act, an employee has the right to;

A safe working environment free of hazards.

Risk assessments, PPE, training, emer gency procedures, and first -aid.

The right to refuse unsafe work and report unhealthy cond itions without fear of reprisal.

Employers must be registered with OSHA, and work injury or disease coverage is provided th rough Workers Compensation Fund.

vi i i . Protection Against Discrimination & Harassment

It's illegal to discriminate based on race, ethnicity, sex, gender, pregnancy, marital/family status, religion, political opinion, disability, HIV/AIDS status, age, or social origin. Harassment is also proh ibited.

viii. Fair Termination

An employee has the right to a fair termination. An employer must abide with the law in terminating his/her employee, such as; reasons for termination must be fair and acceptable before the law, giving notice before termination, severance pay e.t.c.

5 . What is a Contract?

A contract is a mutual agreement between two parties or more, where each party agrees to do something in return for something else from the other party . Parties to contract can be individuals, companies, Organizations or any other entities legally capable of entering into a contract.

6 . What is a Contract of Employment ?

A contract of employment is an agreement between an employer and an employee that sets out the terms and conditions under which the employee will work and the employer will pay for the work. In employment contract , an employee agrees to work for the employer, and the employer agrees to pay the employee for their work. Contract of employment ought to be written , outlining terms such as duties, pay, and conditions. Every employer has a duty to provide an employment contract to their employee.

7 . Types of employment Contract

There are three types of employment contract:

Contract for an Unspecified Period (Permanent/Indefinite Contract)

This is open-ended employment with no fixed end date.

Commonly referred to as permanent employment.

It continues until either the employer or employee terminates it, following proper procedures.

Fixed-Term Contract

The contract has a clearly defined start and end date.

Often used for employees with specific skills or professional expertise, or in administrative positions.

It automatically terminates at the end of the period unless renewed.

Contract for a Specific Task (Contract for a Specific Piece of Work)

The employee is hired to perform a particular task or project.

The contract ends once the task is completed, regardless of the time it takes.

Often used in sectors like construction, consultancy, or project-based roles.

8 . Written Contract Requirements

The employment contract should ensure the following matters:

Voluntary consent without coercion

Terms are legal and understandable

10 . Legal Protections of Oral Employment Contract

Even without a written contract, but the employer and the employee have agreed verbally only, the employee is still protected under the law in terms of:

 Minimum wage

 Leave entitlements

 Termination procedures

 Protection against unfair dismissal

 Overtime compensation

 Health and safety

Because there's no written document, evidence becomes key. Here are ways to prove the existence and terms of an oral contract:

12 . Legal Working Hours

The law clearly outlines the number of days and hours an employee is required to work. According to the law, an employee should not work for more than:

i. 6 days per week ii. 45 hours per week iii. 9 hours per day

NOTE: If an employee is required to work overtime, this must be clearly stated in the employment contract. Overtime must not exceed 50 hours over a four-week cycle. The employer is required to pay the employee for overtime at a rate of not les s than 1½ of th e employee’s salary for each hour of overtime worked.

13 . Leave, Holidays & Time Off

In matters of Leave, Holidays and time off, the law provides as follows :

Annual Leave : At least 28 consecutive days per 12-month leave cycle , including public holidays. Paid as if you were working. Pro rata payable upon termination .

Sick Leave : Up to 126 days per leave cycle . First 63 paid at full wage, next 63 at half wage — with a condition to submit a medical certificate.

Maternity Leave :

84 days (12 weeks) standard, 100 days for multiple birth.

Must notify employer 3 months in advance with medical certificate.

Extra 84 days of maternity leave within her leave cycle, if child dies within one year of birth , and if the employee becomes pregnant and delivered prematurely.

Employers must not assign hazardous work and must allow up to 2 hours/day for breastfeeding.

Paternity Leave :

3 days paid paternity leave (within 7 days of child birth).

7 days paid paternity if born premature birth.

Other Leave :

4 days paid leave for death/sickness of close family members.

14 . Termination of Employment Contract

An employment contract can only be terminated for the following lawful reasons :

a. Misconduct : Theft, insubordination, violence, dishonesty, absenteeism, etc.

b. Poor Performance : After being given support and opportunity to improve .

c. Incapacity : Due to illness , injury , or inability to perform the job .

d. Operational Requirements (Redundancy/Retrenc hment): Based on economic, technological, or structural reasons .

e. Mutual Agreement or Expiry of Contract : Fixed-term contracts naturally expire, or both parties agree to end the contract.

15 . Rules and Procedures for Termination of Emploment Contract

The law requires fair and lawful termination, which must meet two conditions:

i. Substantive Fairness

There must be a valid and fair reason for terminating employment.

ii. Procedural Fairness

The employer must follow due process , including:

Notice of allegations (in writing)

Opportunity to respond

Disciplinary hearing (for misconduct)

Right to representation

Fair and unbiased decision-making process

16 . Notice Periods Before Termination

According to the law, Notice period depends on the duration of the contract as follows;

 Seven (7) days if the notice is given within the first month of employment.

 Four (4) days if the employee is employed on a day-to-day or week-to-week basis.

 Twenty-eight (28) days if the employee is employed on a monthly basis.

NOTE: Payment in lieu (as altenative) of notice is allowed. For example, if the employee was supposed to be given a seven-day notice, instead of that notice, the employer may pay the employee seven days’ salary as a substitute for the notice.

17 . Payments and Compensation Upon Termination

Upon lawful termination, the employee is entitled to the following (depending on the situation):

a. Payment in Lieu of Notice

If proper notice is not given, employer must pay wages equal to the notice period .

b. Severance Pay

• These payments are made to an employee who has been in employment for a period of 12 months or more.

• For a person to be paid these benefits, the reasons for termination must be economic, such as staff reductions to cut employer costs or the employee no longer being needed in their position (Retrenchment or Redundancy).

• This payment is not made to those dismissed for misconduct or poor performance, nor to an employee who has reached retirement age or an employee whose employment contract has expi red or ended due to the passage/expiry of time.

• This payment is equivalent to 7 days of basic salary for each full year of work.

c. Accrued Leave Pay

Employee must be paid for any unused annual leave days.

d. Repatriation Costs

If the employee was recruited from a different location , the employer must cover transportation and relocation costs to their place of recruitment .

e. Terminal Benefits

Includes pension , gratuity , NSSF contributions , and any other contractual entitlements .

18 . Unfair Termination

Under the Employment and Labour Relations Act, termination is considered unfair if:

i. The reason given is not valid or fair , meaning it’s not grounded in:

the employee’s conduct, capacity, or compatibility; or

the employer’s operational requirements

ii. The termination didn’t follow a fair and proper procedure ; such as investigation, disciplinary hearing, opportunity for defense, and adherence to notice requirements .

Additionally, specific circumstances—such as exercising workplace rights, trade union involv ement, pregnancy, or disability cannot serve as lawful reasons for termination .

19 . What an Employee Should Do if They Suspect Unfair Termination ?

a . A ct Quickly

File a complaint with the Commission for Mediation and Arbitration (CMA) within 30 days of termination .

b . Gather and Organize Evidence

Secure any documentation: termination letter, notices, pay slips, correspondence.

Prepare records of hearings, investigations, or disciplinary actions.

Witness statements can be powerful—be sure to collect their names and contact details .

c . Pursue Dispute Resolution

Mediation : Commision for Mediation and Arbitration ( CMA ) first attempts to mediate a resolution.

Arbitration : If mediation fails, then it will proceed to arbitration—also at the CMA. Their decision may include remedies like reinstatement or compensation .

d . Escalate to Labour Court if Needed

If unsatisfied with the CMA’s ruling, you may take the matter to the Labour Division of the High Court , and later to the Court of Appeal if necessary

20 . Remedies for Unfair Termination

If a n arbitrator or court, the Commission for Mediation and Arbitration ( CMA ) or other Labour Court finds termination unfair, they may order:

Reinstatement without loss of pay.

Re -engagement on terms the tribunal decides.

Compensation Payment.

21 . Compensation to Unfair Termination

According to the Labour Laws amendments Act No. 4 of 2025, which amends the employment and Labour Relations Act , compensation scal es have been refined as follows;

in case of termination based on unfair procedure , not less than six (6) months' remuneration but not exceeding 12 months' remuneration;

in case of termination based on unfair reason , not less than 12 months' remuneration but not exceeding 18 months' remuneration;

in case of termination based on both unfair procedure and unfair reason , not less than 12 months’ remuneration but not exceeding 24 months’ remuneration; and 

in case of termination based on discrimination or harassment , not less than 12 months’ remuneration but not exceeding 24 months’ remuneration.
